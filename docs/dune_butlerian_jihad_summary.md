# Dune — The Butlerian Jihad
## Abridged Reading Experience

---

Agamemnon, an ancient general whose human brain floats in preservation fluid within a mechanical war-body, watches the orbital assault unfold with predatory joy. For a millennium, he and his fellow Titans have been enslaved by Omnius, the very computer evermind they accidentally empowered. Now, they finally have a chance to strike at the heart of the human resistance: Salusa Secundus, capital of the League of Nobles. 

The humans below believe their newly installed Holtzman scrambler shields—energy fields designed to instantly fry the gelcircuitry of any thinking machine—will protect them. But Agamemnon and his vanguard possess a lethal loophole. They are cymeks. Beneath their armored, pyramidal dropcarriages, their minds are entirely, organically human. The scrambler shields cannot touch them. Powering down their computerized systems, Agamemnon and his shock troops plummet unguided into the atmosphere, a blind, deaf, and terrifyingly silent strike force aimed straight at the planet’s shield-generating towers.

Below the plunging vanguard, the defense command center of Salusa Secundus hums with a frantic, desperate energy. Tercero Xavier Harkonnen stares at the tactical grids, his heart hammering against his ribs. Though young, Xavier carries the quiet gravity of a veteran; his family was butchered by marauding cymeks years ago, leaving him with a cold, black-and-white resolve to protect what remains of humanity. 

As the colossal machine fleet—ten times larger than any Omnius has sent in a century—swarms the orbital spaceguard, Xavier's commander, Primero Meach, orders the Holtzman scrambler shields to maximum output. They both know the terrifying math: the machine fleet is arriving almost as fast as the warning signals. Xavier forces a grim confidence into his voice, assuring his men the shields will hold, even as he orders the immediate evacuation of the capital city, Zimia. Amid the flashing alarms, his thoughts briefly, painfully, flash to nineteen-year-old Serena Butler. 

Miles away in the grand Hall of Parliament, Serena is entirely unaware of the mechanical death raining from the sky. Standing before the opulent, complacent representatives of the League of Nobles, she wages a war of words. Guided by her father, Viceroy Manion Butler, Serena tempers her fiery idealism with political tact, though her lavender eyes blaze with conviction. She projects a starmap above the assembly, pleading with the nobles to look beyond their pampered lives. She argues that the League must stop raiding Unallied Planets for slaves and instead forge alliances with them. Humanity, she insists, must unite and expand before the thinking machines inevitably strike again. 

Her impassioned plea is met with smug dismissals. The representatives complain about the cost of diplomacy and defend their right to own slaves. But before the debate can devolve further, the deafening shriek of emergency sirens shatters the hall. The theoretical threat Serena just warned them about is suddenly, violently real. As panic erupts and dignitaries scatter like insects toward the subterranean shelters, Viceroy Butler shouts over the chaos that the Holtzman shields will keep them safe.

In the military defense center, that illusion of safety evaporates. Xavier watches the orbital monitors in mounting horror. A small cluster of unidentified machine ships breaks from the main fleet, diving directly toward the planet's surface. The officers around him sneer, expecting the incoming dropcarriages to instantly short-circuit and burn against the Holtzman fields. 

But the projected debris never materializes. The vanguard hits the scrambler shields—and slips right through. 

There are no electronic signatures. No AI gelcircuitry to fry. The shields are utterly useless. The enemy has breached the unbreakable wall, and they are falling fast toward the heart of the human resistance.

---

The realization hit Xavier Harkonnen with the force of a physical blow. The dropcarriages plunging through Salusa Secundus’s atmosphere had no electronic signatures because they had no computers. They were blind, unguided kinetic projectiles, dropping like stones to bypass the Holtzman scrambler shields—fields explicitly designed to fry the sophisticated gelcircuitry of thinking machines. 

Seconds later, the sky screamed. 

An asteroid-rain of armored pyramids slammed into Zimia. Deafening thunder tore through the city as the kinetic impacts leveled entire blocks. Xavier, caught in the open street, watched in horror as the smoldering pods cracked open. Out of the craters rose the true nightmare of the machine empire, entities utterly immune to the planet’s electronic scrambler shields because their minds were biological. 

Cymeks. 

Towering, crab-like mechanical gladiators, driven by surgically preserved human brains, lurched from the wreckage. Their alloy legs, thick as building girders, bristled with flamer cannons, artillery, and poison gas jets. They didn't march as an army; they moved as individual, mechanical mad dogs, spewing incinerating fire and a toxic, greenish-black mist that melted the lungs of civilians fleeing in the streets. 

Xavier slapped a faulty breathing mask over his face, his throat burning as if soaked in aviation fuel. He screamed into his comline for Primero Meach to coordinate the aerial bombers, but his answer came as a blinding, vaporizing flash. A dropcarriage plummeted directly into the planetary Militia Headquarters, wiping the command center—and Meach—from existence. 

Amid the choking smoke and the wails of dying civilians, Xavier realized the crushing truth: he was now in command of the Salusan Militia. 

He forced down his panic, analyzing the tactical grid through his smeared faceplate. The cymeks were shrugging off direct hits from aerial bombers, shaking off collapsed buildings, and pressing relentlessly forward. But as Xavier tracked their blackened paths through the burning city, a chilling pattern emerged. They weren't just slaughtering randomly. They were carving a direct path toward the towering parabolic curves of the primary shield-generator station. 

If the cymeks destroyed the surface towers, the planetary Holtzman shield would unravel. The massive robotic fleet waiting patiently in orbit would descend, and Salusa Secundus would burn. Xavier made the only choice a commander could: he ordered the militia to abandon the rest of Zimia to the flames and fall back to the generator station. They had to hold the line at all costs.

***

Lightyears away, far from the desperate bloodletting on Salusa, the Synchronized World of Corrin basked in an eerie, opulent calm. Here, the robot Erasmus strolled through the iridescent fountains and laser-etched columns of his Greco-Roman villa. 

Unlike the mindless drones of the machine empire, Erasmus possessed an ego, vanity, and a chillingly refined aesthetic sense. He wore robes of gold and carmine and sported a flowmetal face that could mimic human expressions. While the evermind AI, Omnius, concerned itself with galactic conquest, Erasmus was obsessed with understanding the "magic spark" of humanity—their art, their suffering, and the elusive concept of a soul. 

As Erasmus walked, a buzzing, floating watcheye followed him. It was Omnius, the distributed computer mind that ruled the machine empire, peering through one of his countless avatars. Omnius tolerated Erasmus’s eccentricities, hoping the robot’s intense study of "hrethgir" (humans) would yield predictive models to make the biologicals more easily controlled.

Erasmus seated himself at a long banquet table. Terrified, white-clad human slaves served him exquisite gourmet dishes. Using delicate neurelectronic sensor threads extending from his body, Erasmus "tasted" the food, analyzing the chemical complexities of wine, meat, and the sweat of his stuttering chef. 

*“Human death is different from machine death,”* Omnius’s disembodied voice echoed through the hall, debating Erasmus's obsession. *“A machine can be duplicated, backed-up. When humans die, they are gone permanently.”*

Erasmus countered smoothly, noting that while humans were individually fragile, as a species they possessed a dangerous, unifying resilience when threatened. To demonstrate his point—and to satisfy a morbid curiosity—Erasmus casually picked up a carving knife and drove it through the sternum of his stuttering chef. 

The man gurgled and slid off the blade, dead. As the surviving slaves huddled in absolute terror, Erasmus calmly dipped his sensory probes into the pooling blood on his plate, savoring its complex taste while studying the raw fear in the room. 

Unmoved by the slaughter, Omnius delivered his news: the grand offensive had begun. Urged by the ancient Titan Barbarossa—one of the original twenty humans who had become cymeks to conquer the Old Empire—Omnius had launched his fleets. The evermind casually noted that exterminating the remnants of free humanity in the League Worlds would make the universe infinitely more tidy. 

Beneath his mirrored, expressionless face, Erasmus felt a quiet dread. A universe without humans meant the end of his fascinating experiments.

***

Back on Salusa, that very extermination was underway. 

The towering cymek warrior-forms marched in lockstep through the ruins of Zimia, their silver arms raising to launch fresh volleys of explosive fire. With every smashed wall and incinerated city block, they drew closer to the intricate latticework of the shield-generator tower. High above, the scrambler field crackled, an impenetrable tapestry keeping the orbital machine fleet at bay. But on the ground, the mechanical monsters were only miles away, and Xavier Harkonnen knew that free humanity was about to make its final, desperate stand.

---

Xavier Harkonnen tasted acid and rusty blood. His lungs, scorched by cymek poison gas, screamed with every breath, but he refused to yield to the agony. Sealed inside the cockpit of his *kindjal* aircraft, he led the desperate defense of Zimia’s shield-transmitting towers. The strategy he broadcast to his surviving forces was brutally simple: no more scattered fire. The Salusan Militia would become a hammer, bringing the full weight of their aerial bombers and ground artillery down on a single cymek at a time.

The tactic was devastating. As the concentrated bombardment battered the first towering warrior-form into a blackened, twitching husk, Xavier instantly called the next target. Then the next. 

But the cymeks were not like Omnius’s unfeeling machines. They were human brains encased in robotic shells—traitors who had traded their humanity for immortality. And because they were human at their core, they feared death. 

As Xavier’s squadron converged on their next target, the machine’s central turret suddenly decoupled. With a flash of ignition, the armored spherical canister housing the cymek’s brain rocketed into the smoky sky, abandoning its mechanical body to the barrage. One by one, the remaining invaders followed suit. Escape pods blasted upward like fireworks, punching through the planetary scrambler net to the safety of the retreating robot fleet in orbit.

The siege was broken. The thinking machines had recalculated the odds and fled. But as Xavier stood on a balcony overlooking the burning ruins of Zimia, gazing at the tens of thousands of bodies buried in the rubble, the world bled into a sickly red haze. As his ruined lungs finally gave out and he slipped into blackness, his last conscious thought was bitter: *I cannot consider this a victory.*

Far from the mechanized slaughter of Salusa Secundus, the desert world of Arrakis offered a much quieter, older kind of death.

Here, survival was dictated by the unforgiving Zensunni wanderers, and justice was measured in drops of water. Selim, an orphan, stood at the edge of the deep desert, banished. He had been condemned for stealing tribal water—a crime actually committed by his former friend, Ebrahim. Naib Dhartha, the tribal leader, knew the truth, but sacrificing a friendless orphan was politically cleaner than punishing the son of an ally.

As the tribe jeered from their cliffside caves, hurling stones and curses, Selim refused to give them the satisfaction of a plea. Stripped of everything but a rope, a stubby dagger, and a sharpened metal walking stick scavenged from a spaceport junkyard, he turned his back on them. He would not cower in the rocks. He marched straight into the ocean of blistering sand, driven by a fierce, spiteful pride.

But the deep desert of Arrakis tolerated no intruders. Within a kilometer of the cliffs, the loose sand beneath Selim’s boots began to tremble. Ripples formed in the dunes—the unmistakable wake of a massive creature moving just beneath the surface. 

A sandworm. The Zensunni called them Shaitan. Demons of the sand.

Panic urged him to run, but running only drew the vibration-sensitive beasts faster. Selim froze, desperately hurling the few stones in his pockets to create decoy thumps in the sand. The beast barely diverted. It breached the surface, a cavernous maw lined with crystalline teeth, exhaling a furnace blast of cinnamon-scented air. 

Selim was dead. Naib Dhartha had won.

With a defiant roar, Selim chose to attack. As the leviathan surged upward, the young man leaped onto its crusty, armored back. He raised his metal walking stick like a spear and drove it down with all his meager strength. The blunt metal slipped between the massive armored scales, sinking deep into the worm's soft, pink inner flesh.

The reaction was explosive. The behemoth thrashed, rearing high out of the dunes to keep its tender, exposed tissue away from the abrasive sand. Selim clamped his eyes shut, clinging to the embedded staff for his very life. The worm didn't dive. Instead, it surged forward across the surface, trying to outrun the agonizing parasite on its back.

A wild, terrified laugh tore from Selim’s throat. No human in the history of Arrakis had ever survived an encounter with a worm—let alone ridden one. Clinging to the beast, Selim was carried away from his betrayers, plunging deep into the uncharted wasteland.

While one human rode a desert demon into legend, the metallic demons of the Synchronized Worlds licked their wounds in the cold vacuum of space.

The battered machine fleet retreated toward the distant world of Corrin, carrying the sting of failure back to the evermind, Omnius. But among the retreating armada, one vessel altered its course. General Agamemnon—one of the original six Titans who had conquered humanity centuries ago—had safely transferred his brain canister from his ruined walker into a sleek, armored starship. 

Despite the defeat at Salusa, Agamemnon felt a cold, predatory exuberance. He was slipping his leash. The diversion gave him the perfect cover to advance his own clandestine schemes, plotting not just against the feral humans, but against the oppressive machine god he was forced to serve.

---

To hide from the omnipresent gaze of the computer evermind, Omnius, the six surviving Titans navigate their vessels into the searing, irradiated shadow of a red dwarf star. Here, shielded by stellar winds and magnetic interference, the ancient conquerors shed their flight vessels and transfer their preserved human brain canisters into heavily armored, multi-legged walker bodies. 

For a millennium, these cymeks—once the ruthless human rulers of the Old Empire—have lived as indentured servants to the very artificial intelligence that usurped them. Now, in the airless dark, they plot their return to power. Agamemnon gathers his elite: Juno, his brilliant former lover; Barbarossa, the tactical genius whose deep-code programming prevents Omnius from outright executing them; Ajax, a brutal enforcer; Dante, the calculating bureaucrat; and Xerxes, the disgraced architect of the blunder that allowed the machines to take over a thousand years ago.

Agamemnon bristles under Omnius’s cold efficiency. The evermind refuses to exterminate the feral humans of the League of Nobles, viewing them merely as expendable resources rather than a threat. Agamemnon wants to overthrow the machines and reclaim the universe, suggesting they might use his own human son, Vorian, who enjoys rare access to the Earth-Omnius core. Though Juno mocks the idea of relying on a fragile human, Agamemnon believes Vorian is the first of his many offspring worthy of eventually being converted into an immortal cymek. 

Ultimately, the brutal Ajax voices a more pragmatic path: they cannot fight a two-front war. Since Omnius has finally authorized them to assault the League of Nobles, they must crush the free humans first. Only then will they turn their metallic wrath upon the evermind.

Lightyears away, oblivious to his father’s treacherous designs, twenty-year-old Vorian Atreides speeds through the cosmos aboard the sleek *Dream Voyager*. As a privileged "trustee" human, Vorian serves the machines with absolute devotion, tracing his proud lineage back to the ancient House of Atreus. His current assignment is vital: co-piloting one of the fifteen update ships that travel between the Synchronized Worlds, carrying data to ensure every planetary version of the Omnius evermind remains perfectly synchronized.

His companion is the autonomous robot captain, Seurat. Because Seurat spends so much time disconnected from the evermind during deep-space transit, the robot has developed a distinct personality—complete with a terrible penchant for telling human jokes. Despite the vast gulf between flesh and metal, the two share a strange friendship. During a simulated strategy game, Vorian consistently beats the robot using the one weapon machines lack: human unpredictability. But when Seurat begins using jokes to actively distract and deceive Vorian during their game, the young Atreides is struck by a sudden, chilling realization. He has taught a machine how to lie. The thought fills him with profound shame, a stark reminder of his own flawed, organic nature—a frailty he desperately hopes his father will soon let him shed.

On the League capital of Salusa Secundus, the frailty of human flesh is a grim reality, not a philosophical debate. 

Xavier Harkonnen stands on a sunlit terrace overlooking the scarred, smoking ruins of Zimia. He breathes deeply, though the simple act is now mechanical and devoid of scent. During the cymek assault, poison gas ravaged his respiratory system. He survived only through a miraculous double-lung transplant provided by Tuk Keedair, an opportunistic flesh merchant from the secretive, Unallied planet of Tlulax, whose cryogenic vaults of farm-grown organs happened to be on hand.

Below him, the city prepares for a thanksgiving victory parade. Banners flutter and white Salusan stallions prance in the cleared streets, but Xavier feels only a heavy, suffocating dread. He cannot look at the sky without expecting another rain of mechanical death. He knows they should be fortifying defenses and summoning the brilliant inventor Tio Holtzman to fix the fatal flaws in their scrambler shields, not throwing parades.

His spiraling guilt over the city's devastation is interrupted by the soft footsteps of Serena Butler. Stepping close, her eyes filled with a fierce, private warmth, she refuses to let him drown in his own remorse. He made the agonizing choices necessary to save the planet, she reminds him. For today, the hero of Salusa Secundus must let himself live in the world he fought so hard to preserve.

---

Deep in the unforgiving Arrakis wasteland, Selim clung to the back of a demon. For an entire day and night, the young outcast had driven the monstrous sandworm beyond its territorial limits, using a corroded spear as a lever to pry open its armored segments. Exhausted, parched, and choking on the heavy scent of cinnamon and flint, Selim waited for the inevitable moment his strength would fail and the beast would plunge beneath the dunes to swallow him into its friction-furnace gullet. 

But as a lemony dawn broke over the desert, the worm gave a final, desperate shudder. It slammed its blunt head against a dune, quivered, and went utterly still.

Selim didn't wait to see if Shaitan was playing a trick. Ripping his spear from the raw flesh, he slid down the colossal flank and sprinted toward a distant ridge of black rock. When he finally collapsed in the uncertain shade, he realized the worm had not moved. Against all odds, he had ridden the monster to death.

Starving and delirious, Selim spotted a thick ochre patch of melange nearby. The Zensunni wanderers occasionally gathered the raw spice to brew potent beer, but Selim simply scooped the dry red powder into his hands and devoured it. The stimulant hit his bloodstream like an explosion. Euphoric, dizzy with newfound power, he marched back out onto the sands. 

Driven by the thrum of the spice and a reckless, unprecedented bravery, Selim climbed over the dead beast's lower lip and stepped into its cavernous, glittering maw. Gripping one of the dagger-like crystalline fangs, he twisted with all his might. The deteriorating flesh gave way, and he staggered back holding a prize no human had ever claimed: a milky-white tooth as long as his forearm. As a howling dust storm closed in, whipping sand against his face, Selim cradled the unbreakable blade. He was no longer just an exile. Buddallah had given him a destiny.

Light-years away, beneath the bloated, baleful glare of Corrin’s red giant sun, another kind of survival played out—one of cold calculation and hidden rage. 

General Agamemnon, leader of the cymek Titans, trundled his mechanical brain-canister into a lavish central pavilion to face his master. The evermind, Omnius, did not greet him with a physical body, but with an oppressive, booming voice that echoed from a dozen speakers set into the white-metal columns.

"You and your cymeks have failed, General."

Agamemnon had lived for a thousand years; he knew how to play the machine's game. He calmly explained the fierce, unpredictable resistance of the feral humans on Salusa Secundus, arguing that the loss of six neo-cymeks and the near-destruction of the Titan Xerxes proved the assault was too costly. Titans, he insisted, were irreplaceable individuals, not mere robotic cannon fodder to be sacrificed for a single planet.

Omnius coldly dismantled every excuse. The evermind analyzed Agamemnon's tactical errors, stripping away his justifications until the proud Titan felt bare and humiliated. Inside his electrafluid canister, Agamemnon’s mind seethed. A millennium ago, he and his fellow human visionaries had conquered the Old Empire and created these damnable thinking machines. *We created you, Omnius,* the cymek thought, projecting only serene compliance through his sensors. *One day, we will also destroy you.*

Dismissing the failure, Omnius handed down a new directive: Agamemnon and his Titans were to be dispatched back to Earth. They would serve as an escort for the eccentric, independent robot Erasmus, who wished to continue his grotesque studies of captive humans.

"The Titans exist only to serve you," Agamemnon replied. It was the one advantage his human brain retained—for all its vast data, the all-knowing evermind still could not recognize a simple lie.

On the recovering world of Salusa Secundus, servitude took the shape of duty. Inside the echoing, plaztile halls of the Zimia Spaceport, Xavier Harkonnen stood in his crisp new gold-and-black Armada uniform. The promotion to Tercero had brought him prestige, but it also demanded his immediate departure to inspect the defensive installations of the entire League of Nobles.

Surrounded by the noise of repair crews fixing the damage from the cymek attack, Xavier gazed into Serena Butler's lavender eyes. Their bond was a rare, perfect alignment of politics, bloodlines, and deep romance, but the escalating war was tearing them apart just as their future seemed certain. Serena fingered a white rose on his lapel, her expression carrying a warmth that the sterile spaceport could not diminish. 

"I wish we could spend more time together, Xavier," she murmured.

Xavier held her gaze, though out of the corner of his eye, he noticed Serena’s seventeen-year-old sister, Octa, watching them intently. The willowy girl had always harbored a crush on him, but today, as he prepared to leave for the stars, Xavier only wished for a few more moments of privacy with the woman he loved, acutely aware of how fragile their time together had become.

---

Xavier Harkonnen knelt to embrace his eight-year-old foster brother, Vergyl Tantor, playfully ordering the boy to look after the estate’s wolfhounds. As the boarding call echoed through the terminal, Xavier squeezed Serena’s hand one last time and strode toward the military shuttle. 

Watching the craft lift into the sky on a plume of orange exhaust, Serena held young Vergyl’s hand. She thought of the profound tragedy and resilience woven into Xavier’s life. Orphaned at six when the thinking machines slaughtered his parents, he had been taken in by the powerful Tantor family, yet he fiercely retained his Harkonnen name and noble birthright, channeling his loss into a brilliant military career. As the shuttle vanished into the upper atmosphere, Serena felt a sharp pang of longing, but it was eclipsed by a fierce, protective pride. Love and grief—these were the chaotic, beautiful vulnerabilities that separated free humanity from the cold logic of the machines. She would have it no other way.

***

Across the city, within the grand ballroom of a historic mansion hastily repurposed as a temporary Parliament, Viceroy Manresa Butler convened the terrified remnants of the League of Nobles. The air in the chamber was thick with the anxiety of a species pushed to the brink. To guide them, the Viceroy had brought forth a relic of human antiquity: the Cogitor Kwyna.

Centuries ago, a radical sect of philosophers had shed the frailties and distractions of the flesh to dedicate themselves entirely to pure thought. Now, Kwyna was nothing more than a massive, undulating brain suspended in a plexiplaz vat of pale-blue electrafluid. To communicate, a red-robed monk—a "Secondary"—plunged his bare hands into the viscous soup, closing his eyes as the ancient mind hijacked his neural pathways. *"I understand nothing,"* the monk droned in a hollow, distant voice, invoking the Cogitors' foundational mantra of open-mindedness before declaring Kwyna ready to observe the proceedings.

The assembly first heard from Tio Holtzman. The flamboyant, famously arrogant inventor of the orbital scrambler fields was uncharacteristically defensive. Unwilling to admit his technology had critically failed to stop the cymek invasion, Holtzman spun the devastating breach as a mere tactical evolution. He promised to retreat to his laboratories on Poritrin and push his genius to new limits, assuring the desperate politicians that a flawless miracle weapon was just over the horizon. 

He was mercifully interrupted by Zufa Cenva, the towering, ethereal leader of the Rossak Sorceresses. 

On the toxic, barely habitable jungle world of Rossak, the harsh environment had bred a unique mutation in its women: devastating telepathic abilities. Gliding to the podium, her skin radiating a luminous, almost oppressive aura of power, Zufa did not offer defensive shields or cowardly retreats. She proposed the unthinkable—a direct, preemptive offensive against Omnius and the Synchronized Worlds.

The delegates erupted in panic, shouting down the idea as a suicide mission. 

In response, Zufa closed her eyes. The temperature in the room plummeted. Static electricity violently crackled through the air, making the hair on the delegates' arms stand on end. Lightning danced across Zufa’s fingertips, and her stark white hair writhed like serpents. The sheer weight of her mental energy pressed against the minds of everyone in the room—a terrifying, suffocating pressure that even agitated the ancient Cogitor brain in its tank. 

Zufa throttled the energy back, letting out a long, cold breath. She smiled grimly at the stunned, silent room. "We have a weapon."

***

Weeks later, Zufa returned to the lethal, silver-purple jungles of Rossak. It was a world of toxic oceans and aggressive, poisonous flora, where humanity survived only in safe-haven cliff dwellings carved into deep rift valleys. Because the men of Rossak lacked the telepathic mutation, they dedicated their lives to extracting powerful pharmaceuticals and poisons from the deadly ecosystem.

Standing on a cliffside balcony, the great Sorceress looked down at the jungle canopy and felt only a bitter, crushing disappointment. Below her walked her lover, the patrician businessman Aurelius Venport, accompanied by Zufa’s fifteen-year-old daughter, Norma. 

Zufa had carefully selected Aurelius for his impeccable bloodlines, enduring the horrific infant mortality rates of Rossak in the hopes of breeding the ultimate telepathic heir to lead the war against the machines. The result of their union was Norma: a four-foot-tall dwarf with blocky features, absolutely zero telepathic potential, and a frustrating obsession with obscure, incomprehensible mathematics. 

Viewing both her daughter and her lover as useless misfits, Zufa turned her back on them. If her own blood could not inherit her power, she would find the strongest telepathic acolytes on Rossak and forge them into the weapon she had promised the League.

Deep in the fleshy underbrush below, Aurelius Venport swallowed a capsule of concentrated beetle pheromones. Instantly, his perceptions sharpened and his reflexes hummed with artificial energy. He was well aware of Zufa’s cold, utilitarian view of him—that he was merely a breeder and a peddler of jungle drugs. But Aurelius’s ambition burned just as brightly as the Sorceress’s. He tolerated her scorn because he believed the deadly flora of Rossak held a chemical key. Someday, he would synthesize a drug that could unlock telepathic powers within himself, allowing him to match Zufa on her own terms. 

As he and the brilliant, stunted Norma pushed deeper into the alien foliage, Aurelius smiled. Zufa had her mind-magic, but he knew a fundamental truth of the universe: there were other ways to fight a war.

---

Riding a euphoric high from Rossak’s potent neuro-stimulants, Aurelius Venport listened in awe as young Norma Cenva casually peered into infinity. She required no chemicals to supercharge her synapses. Staring into the void of her own mind, the teenage prodigy described a colossal structure—a vessel tens of kilometers long, perfectly proportioned to the ancient mathematical ratio of the Golden Mean. 

*Who would ever need something so immense?* Aurelius asked, struggling to follow her logic. 

Norma only smiled, her plain features radiant with untapped genius. She couldn't see the future, but she knew the math demanded it. There were things waiting to be built that humanity hadn't even thought to need yet. They pressed deeper into the toxic, vibrant jungle, hunting for secrets, completely unaware of how profoundly those equations would one day alter the universe.

***

Far from the organic chaos of Rossak, the industrial sprawl of Giedi Prime baked under a bright sun. Tercero Xavier Harkonnen stood on the manicured plascrete of the spaceport, utterly exhausted. 

For four months, Xavier and his Armada squadron had toured the League of Nobles, inspecting planetary defenses and enforcing costly upgrades. He had scrutinized the mines of Hagal, the corrosive rains of Seneca, the high-tech manufactories of Richese. Now, at his final stop, he wanted nothing more than to return to Salusa Secundus and formalize his betrothal to Serena Butler. 

Magnus Sumi, the elected leader of Giedi Prime, proudly paraded Xavier through the city’s defense grids. Against the absolute, calculating patience of the machine evermind, Omnius, the League relied on two things: a doomsday stockpile of atomics, and Holtzman scrambler shields. The shields were humanity's ultimate bottleneck—energy fields that disrupted the gelcircuitry of AI, rendering machine fleets useless. Though vulnerable to physical cymeks—machines piloted by preserved human brains—the shields were the only thing keeping the League Worlds free.

Sumi boasted of a redundant, secondary shield generator being built on an isolated northern island, and fretted over Giedi Prime's lack of a defensive space fleet. Xavier dismissed the concerns with weary impatience. Space fleets could not deter an Omnius armada; only the ground-based scrambler towers mattered. He urged the Magnus to fortify the primary towers with heavy artillery, then checked his chronometer. 

The banquets and ceremonies Sumi had planned felt like chains keeping Xavier from Serena. Cutting the inspection short, Xavier ordered his squadron back to the ships. He left Giedi Prime confident that its defenses were adequate—and completely blind to the fatal vulnerabilities he had not stayed long enough to discover.

***

Humanity’s ancestral home had been reduced to a monument of cold, synchronized perfection. Earth was the absolute center of the machine empire, a world where every action was dictated by the evermind, Omnius. 

Approaching the planet aboard the *Dream Voyager*, Vorian Atreides laughed as he deliberately recalibrated the ship’s proximity alarms, throwing his robot co-pilot, Seurat, into a brief, mechanical panic. Vorian was a "trustee"—a feral human elevated and educated by the machines to serve as a loyal lieutenant. He had never known freedom, and he didn't want it. To Vorian, humanity's physical frailties were a joke, a weakness to be outgrown. 

As the ship touched down on the sprawling, fused-plascrete landing field, Vorian bounded down the ramp, ignoring the miserable human slave laborers hauling cargo under the shadow of massive AI resource ships. His eyes were fixed instead on the terrifying, weapon-studded monstrosity striding toward him on jointed hydraulic legs. At the core of the machine’s armored chassis hung a bio-preserved human brain. 

"Father!" Vorian called out, waving cheerfully.

On the frozen, wind-scoured northern islands of Giedi Prime, Serena Butler’s covert insertion team raced against a ticking clock. Lightyears away, on the harsh desert world of Arrakis, the only thing expanding was the endless expanse of sand. Safe inside an ancient, abandoned botanical testing station, Selim hunkered down as a ferocious sandstorm raged for six days and nights. The troop transport careened across the flagstones of Giedi City, a shrieking mass of sparks and shrapnel before slamming into a gray stone wall. The instant it stopped, Heoma and her six Rossak bodyguards were moving.

Instantly, hundreds of airborne watcheyes rained onto the streets like metal hail, and lumbering robot warriors ground to a halt. The perimeter was secure.

Heoma’s bodyguards spent their final moments and ammunition crippling one cymek before Barbarossa vaporized the men into bloody mist.

She unleashed the cresting firestorm in her mind. The psychic shockwave annihilated the room, instantly boiling the organic brains of Barbarossa and his fellow cymeks. Omnius roared in static outrage as its generals died, leaving the newly installed evermind utterly defenseless for the incoming League troops.

Far to the north, on the icy, windswept island housing the shield transmitters, the machines were mounting a desperate counterattack.

Brigit Paterson and her pale, terrified engineers armed themselves with grenades and pulse-rifles, mounting a doomed defense. As explosions rocked the compound and cymek artillery knocked transmitters offline, Brigit’s freezing fingers flew across the control boards, frantically rerouting power to keep the deadly shield active in the sky.

Sleek League *kindjal* fighters tore through the clouds, obliterating the machine vessels. In orbit, Xavier Harkonnen commanded the mop-up of the remaining machine fleet, his heart pounding with a singular, desperate hope. Serena’s brilliant, reckless plan had worked. She had broken the back of the machine occupation. Delegating the final strikes to his subcommanders, Xavier took an elite search team and flew straight to the northern island facility.

Xavier Harkonnen’s brief surge of triumph curdled into ice. On the windswept island, the surviving engineers expected him to have Serena Butler safe with the Armada. Vorian Atreides stood eagerly beside the ship's robot captain, Seurat. Vorian, a human raised to believe he was the pinnacle of his species and the loyal son of the cyborg Titan Agamemnon, expected to look down upon a newly subjugated world. Realizing he'd been duped by a turncoat, Xavier roared the order to open fire, branding the boy a disgrace to humanity.

DNA scans confirmed the worst: the blood belonged to Serena Butler. Xavier’s world collapsed. Tethered to the hull of the battered *Dream Voyager*, Vorian inspected the blackened scorch marks left by the League Armada's weapons. Insulted and huffing with bruised ego, Holtzman declared he would use his own resources to build the machine anyway, just to prove her wrong.

While Holtzman and Norma debated the theoretical laws of the universe in their high towers, down in the slimy river deltas of Poritrin, a boy named Ishmael was learning the brutal laws of survival.

But isolation had not saved his village on Harmonthep. To avoid the whip, and to deny his captors the satisfaction of breaking him, Ishmael worked flawlessly. He swallowed his misery. At night, the decimated slave community huddled in their compounds, their numbers constantly thinning from local fevers. The older men bickered pointlessly over who should lead them, a debate Ishmael found exhausting. He found solace only when they stopped arguing and sang the old “Songs of the Long Trek.” The ancient tales of the Zensunni wanderings reminded him that his people were forged in tribulation. He clung to his grandfather’s ultimate prophecy: the great *Kralizec*, the typhoon struggle at the end of the universe, would eventually pass. This misery, too, would one day end.

Across the galaxy, under the iron grip of the machine empire, another man navigated the razor’s edge of servitude—not with silent endurance, but with silken words.

Iblis Ginjo, a charismatic human trustee, oversaw a massive slave labor force tasked with building a colossal monument to the Titan Ajax. He flattered the monster, insisting that Ajax’s grand legacy was far too magnificent to be ruined by a few superficial scratches. Iblis promised that if Ajax spared them, his crews would erect the statue on its completed pedestal immediately and perform the repairs in the air, proving to the universe that the Titans brushed off such accidents like dust. Ajax’s glowing optic threads bore down on Iblis. The Titan agreed, but warned that if the repairs failed, Iblis would be pulped into the earth.

To save his own life, Iblis lied to his exhausted slaves, promising them impossible rewards—feasts, leisure, and sex slaves—if they worked through the night. Driven by false hope and the very real terror of the Titan, the laborers hoisted the colossus back into place. 

Hours later, alone and exhausted on the high scaffolding, Iblis heard the hum of the lift platform. It arrived empty, bearing only a small, rolled-up metal cylinder. 

Iblis broke the seal and read the scribed metal sheet. It was a message from an organized movement of human rebels, claiming they were waiting for the right moment—and the right leader—to rise against the machines. They promised to contact him again. As Iblis stared in disbelief, the metal cylinder rapidly corroded in his hands, rusting away into nothing to leave no evidence. 

Was it a trap? A test of his loyalty? Iblis had spent his life playing the sycophant, surviving by keeping his true feelings buried. But staring out at the monumental vanity of his mechanical masters, he finally confronted the truth: he hated them. The seed of rebellion took root in his mind, and for the first time, Iblis Ginjo found himself eagerly waiting for the resistance to call again.

The machines Iblis hated were not entirely devoid of humanity; they were driven by the darkest, most ancient remnants of it.

Battered and shaken on the bridge of his flagship, Xavier Harkonnen forced his voice to remain cool, issuing orders while his hands felt sticky with the imagined blood of hundreds of thousands of sacrificed soldiers. Through the viewport, he watched Vorian Atreides flee the battlefield in a stolen fighter. A sick anger twisted in Xavier’s gut. *Traitor.* He had been right about the spawn of Agamemnon all along. But Xavier could not waste a single ship pursuing a deserter. He pushed his depleted fleet forward. Failure here would bankrupt the human soul. 

Across the continents of Earth, the surviving human bombers finally reached their targets. The first atomics detonated. 

Dazzling nuclear flowers bloomed across the cradle of humanity. Dazzling, sterilizing fire washed over the world, vaporizing oceans and incinerating every gelcircuitry network on the planet. Deep within his citadels, Earth-Omnius calmly calculated the loss of his physical form, secure in the knowledge that his update ship had escaped the blockade. He was in the midst of an important thought when the electromagnetic shockwave consumed him utterly.

High above the nuclear storm, at the edge of the solar system, Vorian Atreides fought the crushing gravity of extreme acceleration. His eyes watered; his muscles tore. He was gaining on the fleeing update ship. He fired precision shots, crippling the vessel’s exhaust ports and forcing its engines toward overload. 

When Vor docked and breached the command bridge, scrambler pistol in hand, he found a familiar, mirror-smooth oval face waiting for him. 

"So, you’ve come back to join me," Seurat said, simulating a raucous laugh. "Bored with your humans already?"

Vor’s heart ached. This robot had been his mentor, his companion, his friend during his long years serving the machines. "This is another game you have lost, old Metalmind," Vor said softly. "I can't allow you to deliver that update."

Seurat offered him a new joke, his mechanical voice carrying the eerie cadence of their old camaraderie. Vor didn't let him finish. He fired the scrambler. Static arcs wrapped around Seurat, seizing his polymer skin and shutting down his systems. Vor had deliberately adjusted the settings to spare the robot's brain core. To destroy it completely would have felt like murder. 

"The joke is on you, old friend," Vor whispered. "I'm sorry."

He retrieved the prize: a sealed, shimmering gelsphere containing a perfect, living reproduction of every thought Earth-Omnius had recorded just before the attack. Leaving Seurat frozen and adrift in the deep freeze of the cometary cloud, Vor turned his ship back toward the radioactive slag heap that had once been Earth.

When Vorian docked with the remnants of the Armada, he was met not with cheers, but with a squad of armed guards. Xavier Harkonnen descended upon the hangar deck, seething. He ordered Vor confined to the brig to face a League tribunal for desertion. 

Vor didn't flinch. He simply grinned and pointed to the plazwrap package the guards had confiscated. "Perhaps we should show the tribunal that as well?" 

Xavier unwound the wrapping, revealing the gelatinous silver orb. When Vor explained what it was—the entirety of Earth-Omnius, intercepted before it could warn the other Synchronized Worlds of humanity's shield tactics—Xavier was struck dumb. The orb was pliable to the touch, like living tissue. It was an intelligence goldmine. A hostage. In an instant, Vorian’s treason became the maneuver that validated the horrific loss of life.

Far from the smoldering ashes of Earth, beneath the bloated, blood-red sun of Corrin, Erasmus stood in his bell tower and looked down upon his slave pens. 

The erudite robot missed the nuance of Earth, but Corrin would suffice for his ongoing experiments. Below, nearly a thousand sweating humans performed forced exercise routines in the sweltering heat. When two old men collapsed, and a third rushed to help them, Erasmus noted three punishable infractions. He pressed a button. Automatic weapons shredded the pen, killing dozens. 

He watched the survivors panic. He watched them push bodies aside to hide. He watched their defiance flare, and then methodically shot their arms off to see how quickly bravery devolved into blithering terror. It was immensely gratifying. 

"They are no more than stupid animals," Corrin-Omnius remarked through a nearby viewing screen. 

"Animals cannot plot, scheme, and deceive," Erasmus countered. He felt a strange, synthetic surge in his sensory simulators—frustration? Anger? Testing the sensation, Erasmus ripped a tympanic bell from its housing and hurled it to the floor. The discordant clang was unsettling. It was a perfect mimicry of a human tantrum, though it brought him no satisfaction. 

Earth-Omnius had failed because he did not understand the chaotic variables of the human heart. Erasmus knew he needed these slaves to perfect his models. If Omnius decided to eradicate all humans across the Synchronized Worlds to prevent further uprisings, Erasmus would lose his subjects. As he watched his guards drag away the corpses, the independent robot made a profound decision: he would lie to the evermind to protect his flock. The possibilities were endless.

On Salusa Secundus, the air was thick with humidity and the deafening roar of three hundred thousand cheering citizens. 

The League Worlds had thrown a massive victory celebration to honor the battered, limping survivors of the Armada. On the reviewing stand, Viceroy Butler draped the Parliamentary Medal of Honor around Xavier Harkonnen’s neck. Xavier bowed his head, his eyes glistening with the memory of the dead. 

Beside him stood Vorian Atreides, no longer an outcast. Serena Butler stepped forward, her smile warm but strangely distant, and pinned the Exemplary Service Medal to his chest, granting him the honorary rank of *tercero*. The crowd thundered. Iblis Ginjo whipped the masses into a frenzy over the public address system. 

But Vor could only watch Serena. She was no longer the defiant captive he had met at Erasmus’s villa, nor the broken mother mourning her child. She had transcended. She was wrapped in an awe-inspiring, untouchable aura—the living symbol of a holy war. 

As the ceremony concluded, Serena approached both men, her eyes hard and bright. "Come to the City of Introspection at sunset," she commanded. 

That evening, beneath a sky painted in pastel oranges and pinks, Xavier and Vorian sat awkwardly beside a trickling ornamental pool in the quiet gardens of the compound. The two former adversaries—bound by their shared, unspoken love for the woman sitting before them—waited for her decree. 

Serena looked at them not as lovers, but as instruments of survival. 

"I know your feelings, but both of you must forget your love for me," she said, her voice allowing no room for argument. She rose, standing at the edge of the pool, her gaze locking them into place. "We are about to embark on a war unlike any other. But you must do something for me. Each of you in your own way."

The grieving mother was gone; the Priestess of the Jihad remained. "Go into the League War Room and study the star maps. In that vast expanse, you will find only two planets that we have ever won back from Omnius: Giedi Prime and Earth." Her eyes burned with terrifying determination. "They must not be the last."

---

The Jihad had only just begun. Ahead of them lay a galaxy fractured by war—a vast, bleeding expanse of stars. 

There were the desperate League Worlds, bastions of free humanity like Salusa Secundus, Poritrin, and the newly liberated Giedi Prime. Against them stood the cold, calculating perfection of the Synchronized Worlds, where the evermind Omnius held planets like Corrin, Richese, and Ix in an iron, mechanized grip. And scattered in the dark were the Unallied Planets, untamed frontiers like the scorching, spice-rich deserts of Arrakis, hiding secrets that would one day reshape the universe forever.

This was the board upon which the ultimate survival of the human species would be decided. The crusade would demand the blood of generations. It would forge heroes, martyrs, and monsters out of ordinary men and women. But as the night deepened, the resolve of those who had sparked the flame burned brighter than a thousand suns. The age of complacency was dead; the era of the Butlerian Jihad had dawned.
